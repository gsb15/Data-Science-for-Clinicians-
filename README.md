# Data-Science-for-Clinicians-
**Course Timeline:** This is a two-day course, constituting 16 hours of material. There are pre-course materials that are designed for all students to complete prior to attending the course. These pre-course materials are designed for student success upon attendance and are highly recommended. 

**Course Rational:** Current clinical medicine requires professionals who are confident and skilled in gathering, managing, analyzing, and interpreting data. This new clinical skillset requires data science core competencies including computer coding, data cleaning, data visualization, and data synthesis. However, current data science knowledge and skills in clinical training are sparse. This course enhances the medical curriculum by filling this clinical training gap where there is currently no formal training course. 

**Course Description:** Data Science for Clinicians is designed to teach basic data science, epidemiological analyses, and clinical synthesis skills to clinical medical students and professionals to prepare them for modern medical practice. This course will be taught using open-access computer language software and publicly available datasets from different disciplines, to increase the transparency and sustainability of this program. This course is designed to couple knowledge and application to ensure participants can apply the course objectives. This curriculum provides the tools necessary to ask critical questions, training to think in data driven questions, and skills to synthesize individual patient and health system level results. This course will be taught with a mix of lectures and practicals covering the entire data science pipeline from data acquisition to presentation to clinical synthesis. The course will consist of two days of work.

**Data Sets:** All datasets used for this course are open source and freely accessible. They can be downloaded and shared, to explore these data. From experience, the more exploration and collaboration that occurs while learning will improve burgeoning data science skills. 

•	Four Professional Leagues Data: This is web scraped data from the four major professional sport leagues in North America. These data are injury surveillance and transactions data from 2007-2019. The data can be found here (under Supplement 1): https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-87920-6#Sec28   

•	National Basketball League (NBA) Data: This is web scraped data of injury surveillance and performance data from the NBA from 2007-2020. The overall NBA data can be found here: https://osf.io/xywq4/. The NBA rookies data can be found here: https://github.com/gsb15/NBA_Rookies_Data. 

•	‘Running Through’ Data: These data are from a cohort of recreational runners from across Europe from 2020-2022, using weekly reporting, Garmin watch, and Strava, to obtain weekly running exposure and injuries. The data can be found here: https://osf.io/jgfau/. 


**Tools:** There are specific computing and online tools required for this course. All are open access and freely accessible. 
•	R Computer Software: This course will be taught using R computer software. You can download the software here for MAC of PC: https://www.r-project.org/.

•	R Studio: It is recommended to use R Studio as your GUI interface, instead of the basic R software. In my experience, it is easier, quicker, and more intuitive. You can download R studio for MAC or PC here: https://posit.co/download/rstudio-desktop/.

**Course Objectives/Outcomes:** 
At the completion of the course, students will: 
1. Adhere strictly to ethical guidelines regarding participant consent, conflicts disclosure,
data transparency and framing of patient projections communicated to key knowledge users.

2. Articulate methodology assumptions, strengths, and gaps transparently to allow
independent expert scrutiny prior to supplying any data reports or analyses to clinicians, healthcare leaders, and/or researchers. 

3. Visualize different data sources into best scientific practice for effective communication to clinicians, healthcare leaders, and/or researchers.
 
4. Identify, communicate, and control for confounding in clinical based questions. 

5. Calculate prevalence, incidence, and rates and communicate the different clinical inferences of these epidemiological calculations. 

6. Manage uncertainty bias, overfitting limitations, data leakage, and algorithmic bias to improve reliability and generalizability of patient data analyses.

7. Appraise best practices for prediction framework conceptualization spanning research
inquiry, methodology, implementation, and responsible communication of patient
projections.
